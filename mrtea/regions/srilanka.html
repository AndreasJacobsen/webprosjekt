Introduction:

If you think ‘the pearl of the Indian ocean’, also known as Sri Lanka, would host the perfect conditions for tea-growers, you’d only be partially right.
In this page Mr. Tea will tell you the story about Sri Lanka and its relations to the tea plant and their history together.
If you keep on reading he will even tell you how Toy Story have (in a very vague way) relations to Sri Lanka’s tea history.

History:

Sri Lanka got the tea-plant rather late historically. Even though they in 1995 were the world’s second largest tea exporter, and in 2007 stood for almost 8% of all the production of tea in the world, Sri Lanka didn’t get their first tea plant until 1852.
This is rather late considering their close relation to India which was one of the first countries to produce the plant after China, as early as the 17th century.

Now to the relation to Toy Story and Sri Lankans tea history. It was an exporter named James Taylor who had the glory of introducing the tea-plant to Sri Lanka. If you don’t see the connection yet, fear not. James Taylor is also the name of a singer/songwriter who is responsible for the song ‘You got a friend in me’. I bet by now you see the connection, if not here it is. ‘You got a friend in me’ is the title song of the movie Toy Story which in one of Pixar’s biggest movie hits. Now back to history.

As mentioned above James Taylor imported the first batch of tea plants for commercial use. The first plant was introduced to the country in 1825, but this was not for commercial use.
During the time from colonization to importing the plant they discovered that Sri Lanka’s humidity, cool temperatures and rainfall was only in favor of the upmost highest quality of tea species.
This was a fact Taylor was aware of, which he used to import only high quality tea, today named the same as the colony of Sri Lanka, Ceylon.

Current state:

To look at all of this with a positive outcome, you could say that thanks to the British colonization the Sri Lankans are grossing more than 700 million dollars annually (2% of GDP), and employing more than 1 million people.

Today Sri Lanka stands as the 4th biggest producer of tea and was in 1995 the world’s biggest exporter with 23% of the world’s export of tea.

Russia is without any doubt the biggest importer of Sri Lankan tea with 46 million kilos of tea imported each year. In comparison Syria comes second with 21 million kilos imported.

Tea types:

There is mainly 3 types of Sri Lankan tea. Everyone is a variant of Ceylon. To make sure that you get the real deal, look for the Ceylon Tea symbol of quality.
This means that the tea has been grown and packed in Ceylon,
‘the pearl of the Indian ocean’ or Sri Lanka whichever you prefer.

Ceylon Black tea

This is Sri Lanka’s specialty. It has an aroma close reminiscent of citrus, and can be obtained in a pure form or in a blend. If you have ever tasted an earl grey tea it was most probably containing Ceylon Black tea as its main ingredients. (http://ratetea.com/style/ceylon-black-tea/69/)

Ceylon Green tea

This type of Ceylon is manly grown in the Idalgashinna province. It has a rather full body with taste of nuts and malt.
It has a taste reminiscent of traditional Chinese gunpowder tea.
The green tea plays a very small part in the overall production of tea in Sri Lanka and has an acquired taste.

[To Sri Lanka-top]

Ceylon White tea

Ceylon white tea also plays a small role in the overall production of tea in Sri Lanka.
But this type of tea is a bit more exclusive. The tea is grown, harvested and rolled by hand with dried leaves and rested in the sun to dry up.
It has a taste of light liquor, pine, honey and an infusion of copper. The fact that all of this is done by hand is also reflected in the price.
Keep that in mind if you are in a country and not completely aware of the currency exchange.
